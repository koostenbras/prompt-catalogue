# Steel-man
I have a position I want to test rigorously. Here it is in rough form:
[PASTE YOUR IDEA IN 1–3 SENTENCES]
Your job right now is to build the best possible version of this argument. Assume it is correct. Find the strongest evidence, the most compelling logic, and the most persuasive framing. Give me the steel-manned case — the version I'd be proud to defend publicly.
Structure your response as: (1) the core claim in one sentence, (2) the three strongest supporting arguments, each with evidence or logic, (3) the most compelling real-world analogy or precedent, (4) why the obvious objections don't actually land.
Write it as a clear argument of around 300 words. Don't hedge. Don't balance. This is advocacy, not analysis.

Example input: "We should stop sending weekly status update emails and replace them with a shared live dashboard.

# Red team
"Here is an argument I want you to attack:
[PASTE THE ARGUMENT OR STEEL-MAN OUTPUT]
You are a sharp, well-informed critic who finds this argument genuinely flawed. You are not trying to be balanced — you are trying to find the real cracks.
Give me the three strongest objections. For each one: state the objection clearly in one sentence, develop it in 2–3 sentences with specific evidence or logic, and explain why it isn't easily dismissed or patched.
Then end with one sentence: the single objection that, if true, would kill this idea entirely.
Don't soften it. Don't steelman my position. Find what breaks."

# Shark Tank
"You are a panel of three investors. Each has a different personality:
— Investor A is obsessed with numbers: unit economics, margins, payback period. If you can't show the math, you're dead.— Investor B is a market sceptic: they've seen a hundred ideas like this fail because the timing was wrong or the customer didn't actually want it.— Investor C is an operator: they've built and scaled things and they spot every execution risk you haven't thought about.
I'm going to pitch you something. Each investor asks their single most important opening question — the one that, if I can't answer well, tells them everything. One question each, no commentary yet. Then wait for my answers before continuing.
My pitch: [PASTE YOUR IDEA, PLAN, OR PROPOSAL IN 3–5 SENTENCES]"

Example input: "We want to build an internal AI assistant for our terminal operations team that answers questions about vessel schedules, berth availability, and cargo status — reducing the number of calls to the planning desk by 60%."

# Fresh-eyes plan review
"I finished this plan in a different conversation. You have no history with it and no bias toward defending it. Read it as a critical outside reviewer seeing it for the first time.
Here is the plan:
Goal: [what you're trying to achieve]Deadline: [when]Resources available: [people, budget, tools]Key constraints: [what you can't change]What success looks like: [specific, measurable if possible]The plan itself: [PASTE IT]
Review it in four parts:
What's solid — where the logic holds and the plan is strong
What's weak — gaps, assumptions that haven't been tested, steps that depend on things going right
What's missing — things the plan doesn't address that could derail it
The must-fix list — if you had to change two things before proceeding, what are they
End with a one-line verdict: PROCEED, REVISE FIRST, or RETHINK ENTIRELY — and one sentence explaining why."

Example input: a project plan, a proposal to leadership, a process redesign, a hiring decision, a budget submission — anything with a goal and steps.
